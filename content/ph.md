# pH

## Definition

pH measures how acidic or basic the water is, on a scale from 0 to 14. In a reef
tank it is driven mainly by the balance of dissolved CO₂ and carbonate: more CO₂
pushes pH down, off-gassing CO₂ lets it rise. Because it tracks the same
carbonate system as alkalinity, pH and alkalinity are best read together.

## Measurement & Units

pH is **unitless**. Measure it with a calibrated **pH probe/meter** or a liquid
test kit. It swings naturally over the day — lowest before lights-on, highest
late in the photoperiod — so note *when* you test and compare like-for-like.
Calibrate probes regularly with fresh reference solutions.

## Ideal Range

**8.0–8.4**, with most reefs sitting comfortably around 8.1–8.3. A stable value
in this band matters more than chasing a single number; a steady 8.1 beats a
value that lurches between 7.8 and 8.4 each day.

## When It's Too High

Sustained pH above ~8.5 can stress fish and invertebrates and can force calcium
and alkalinity out of solution (abiotic precipitation), clouding the water and
scaling equipment. It usually comes from over-driving a CO₂-scrubbing setup or
excessive aeration paired with heavy dosing.

## When It's Too Low

Low pH (below ~7.9) is most often caused by **excess CO₂** — a closed, poorly
ventilated room, or too little gas exchange. It can slow coral calcification and
leave corals looking sluggish, with polyps less extended.

## Signs & Symptoms

- Corals slow to grow or with poor polyp extension despite good alkalinity.
- Fish breathing hard or lethargic when pH is far off.
- A wide daily swing (>0.3) pointing to CO₂/aeration problems.

## Suggested Remedies

- **To raise:** improve gas exchange — open a window, add a fresh-air line to the
  skimmer, or run a **CO₂ scrubber**. Running a refugium on a reverse-daylight
  schedule steadies pH overnight.
- **To lower:** ease off aeration/scrubbing and confirm alkalinity dosing isn't
  overshooting.
- Prioritize **stability** over the exact figure, and always interpret pH
  alongside alkalinity.
