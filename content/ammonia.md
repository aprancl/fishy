# Ammonia

## Definition

Ammonia (NH₃/NH₄⁺) is the first and most toxic waste product of the nitrogen
cycle, released by fish respiration, waste, and decaying food or organisms. In an
established tank, beneficial bacteria convert it to nitrite and then nitrate
almost as fast as it appears, so a healthy reef reads effectively zero.

## Measurement & Units

Measured in **parts per million (ppm)** with a colorimetric test kit. Test
during **cycling**, after adding livestock, or any time something dies or fish
look distressed. A reading above zero in an established tank is an alarm, not a
routine data point.

## Ideal Range

**0 ppm** (undetectable). Any persistent measurable ammonia means the biological
filter cannot keep up and livestock are at risk.

## When It's Too High

Ammonia is acutely **toxic** — it burns gills and skin and can kill fish and
invertebrates quickly. Toxicity rises with higher pH and temperature, so a spike
during warm, high-pH conditions is especially dangerous.

## When It's Too Low

There is no "too low" — zero is the goal. A steady zero simply means your
nitrifying bacteria are keeping pace with the tank's bioload.

## Signs & Symptoms

- Fish gasping at the surface, clamped fins, rapid gill movement, or lethargy.
- A sudden reading after adding livestock, overfeeding, or an unnoticed death.
- Cloudy water accompanying a bioload spike.

## Suggested Remedies

- **Immediately** do a water change to dilute it, and remove any uneaten food or
  dead organisms.
- Stop or drastically cut feeding until the reading returns to zero.
- Add a bottled nitrifying-bacteria supplement and/or use an ammonia-binding
  conditioner to protect livestock while the biofilter catches up.
- Investigate the cause — overstocking, overfeeding, or a stalled cycle — so it
  does not recur.
