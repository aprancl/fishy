# Nitrate

## Definition

Nitrate (NO₃⁻) is the end product of the nitrogen cycle — the relatively
harmless form that ammonia and nitrite are converted into. Unlike its
predecessors, a small, steady amount of nitrate is **beneficial**: it is a
nutrient corals use, and it forms one half of the nutrient pair with phosphate.

## Measurement & Units

Measured in **parts per million (ppm)** with a colorimetric or digital test kit.
Test **weekly** and read it together with phosphate — the balance of the two
matters more than either number alone.

## Ideal Range

**1–10 ppm** for a mixed reef. SPS-dominant systems often aim for the low end
(~1–5 ppm); LPS and softies tolerate more. As with phosphate, the target is
low-but-present, not zero — starved nutrients cause their own problems.

## When It's Too High

Excess nitrate fuels **nuisance algae and cyanobacteria**, can brown-out or
darken coral coloration, and often signals overfeeding or under-powered export.
Very high levels stress sensitive corals.

## When It's Too Low

Driving nitrate to zero **starves corals** of a needed nutrient. It commonly
causes pale or bleaching corals and is a frequent trigger for **dinoflagellate**
outbreaks, which thrive in nutrient-depleted water.

## Signs & Symptoms

- Hair algae or cyano spreading when nitrate runs high.
- Corals darkening/browning from excess nutrients.
- Pale, "washed out" corals or a dino bloom when nitrate is bottomed out.

## Suggested Remedies

- **To lower:** increase export — larger/more frequent water changes, better
  skimming, less feeding, a refugium (macroalgae), or carbon dosing (vodka/vinegar
  or a commercial product). Lower it gradually.
- **To raise:** feed a bit more, reduce export, or dose a nitrate supplement to
  lift it off zero.
- Keep nitrate and **phosphate in balance**, aiming for steady low levels rather
  than an absolute zero.
