# Nitrite

## Definition

Nitrite (NO₂⁻) is the second stage of the nitrogen cycle: bacteria convert
ammonia into nitrite, and a second group then converts nitrite into far less
harmful nitrate. It is a transient middle step that should be undetectable in an
established reef.

## Measurement & Units

Measured in **parts per million (ppm)** with a colorimetric test kit. It is most
relevant while **cycling a new tank** — watching nitrite rise and then fall to
zero is the classic sign the cycle is completing — and as a check after any
ammonia spike.

## Ideal Range

**0 ppm** (undetectable). A measurable level in an established system signals an
incomplete or disrupted nitrogen cycle.

## When It's Too High

Nitrite is less acutely lethal in saltwater than in freshwater, but sustained
levels still **stress fish** and indicate the biofilter isn't fully established.
Persistent nitrite usually accompanies, or follows, an ammonia problem.

## When It's Too Low

Zero is the target; there is no downside to an undetectable reading. It means
both stages of nitrification are keeping up with the tank.

## Signs & Symptoms

- Elevated nitrite during or shortly after cycling a new tank.
- Fish stress (lethargy, reduced appetite) alongside a recent ammonia spike.
- A reading that appears after the biofilter is disturbed (media swap, medication).

## Suggested Remedies

- Perform **water changes** to bring the level down while bacteria catch up.
- Ease off feeding and stocking until nitrite returns to zero.
- Dose a nitrifying-bacteria supplement to reinforce the second-stage bacteria.
- Be patient during cycling — nitrite naturally rises, then falls to zero as the
  cycle finishes.
