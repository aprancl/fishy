# Alkalinity

## Definition

Alkalinity is the water's **buffering capacity** — its ability to resist swings
in pH. In a reef tank it is largely carbonate and bicarbonate, the same building
blocks corals use to lay down their skeletons. Because it is consumed as corals
grow, alkalinity is often the fastest-moving of the "big three" (alkalinity,
calcium, magnesium) and the one most worth watching.

## Measurement & Units

Measured in **degrees of carbonate hardness (dKH)** with a titration test kit;
some kits report **meq/L** instead (1 meq/L ≈ 2.8 dKH). Titrate a measured water
sample drop by drop until it changes color, then read the result. Test at the
same time of day when possible, since alkalinity can dip slightly overnight.

## Ideal Range

**8–9 dKH** for a stable mixed reef. The broader acceptable range is roughly
**7–11 dKH**, but consistency beats the exact figure: a rock-steady 8 dKH is far
better for corals than a value that bounces between 7 and 10.

## When It's Too High

High or rapidly-rising alkalinity can **burn** the tips of sensitive SPS corals,
causing tissue recession and a "burnt tip" look. It can also drive pH up and,
paired with high calcium, encourage abiotic precipitation that clouds the water
and clogs equipment.

## When It's Too Low

Low alkalinity leaves the tank poorly buffered, so **pH becomes unstable** and
swings easily. Coral skeletal growth slows or stalls, and combined with a drop
in calcium/magnesium it stresses the whole system. Low alkalinity frequently
signals that coral consumption has outpaced dosing.

## Signs & Symptoms

- Receding or "burnt" SPS tips (too high) or dull, slow-growing corals (too low).
- pH that drifts or swings more than usual (low buffering).
- A steady daily decline that tracks how fast your corals are growing.

## Suggested Remedies

- **To raise:** dose a carbonate/bicarbonate alkalinity supplement (or soda ash),
  spread across small amounts to avoid a spike.
- **To lower:** pause dosing and let coral consumption draw it down, or do a
  water change with correctly-mixed salt.
- Aim to change alkalinity no faster than **~0.5–1 dKH per day**.
- Once demand is known, dose **consistently** (manual daily doses or a doser) and
  keep calcium and magnesium in balance so alkalinity holds steady.
